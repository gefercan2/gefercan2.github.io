Who has won it most?
In a quiet world defined by repetition and refinement, mastery emerges through precision, patience, and time.
By Editorial Studio •
Photography by Placeholder • April 2026

The Copa Libertadores is not only defined by its drama and passion but also by the individuals and institutions that have shaped its legacy. Over more than six decades, certain clubs, managers, and goalscorers have risen above the rest, establishing records that define excellence in South American football. These figures are not merely statistical leaders; they represent eras of dominance, tactical innovation, and extraordinary individual talent. Examining the most successful teams, coaches, and goalscorers in the history of the Copa Libertadores offers a deeper understanding of how greatness is built and sustained in one of the world’s most demanding competitions.The origins of the Copa Libertadores can be traced back to the late 1950s, when South American football authorities sought to create a continental competition that would determine the best club side in the region. Inspired by the success of the European Cup, which had begun in 1955, the South American Football Confederation (CONMEBOL) launched the inaugural tournament in 1960. The name “Libertadores” pays homage to the liberators of South America—figures such as Simón Bolívar and José de San Martín—who led independence movements against colonial rule. This symbolic naming reflects the tournament’s deeper cultural significance, linking football to themes of identity, pride, and resistance. The first edition featured just seven teams, including champions from countries like Uruguay, Argentina, and Brazil, and was ultimately won by Uruguay’s Peñarol, setting the stage for decades of intense competition. From the perspective of club success, no team stands taller than Independiente. The Argentine side has won the Copa Libertadores seven times, making it the most successful club in the competition’s history . What makes Independiente’s achievement particularly remarkable is the period in which their dominance occurred. During the late 1960s and early 1970s, the club established itself as an almost unstoppable force, winning four consecutive titles between 1972 and 1975. This era cemented their nickname, “Rey de Copas” (King of Cups), and set a benchmark that no other club has surpassed. Their success was built on a combination of tactical discipline, technical ability, and a winning mentality that thrived under pressure. Even though the club has not reached similar heights in recent decades, its historical legacy remains unmatched.

Close behind Independiente is Boca Juniors, with six Libertadores titles . Boca’s success spans multiple eras, reflecting its ability to reinvent itself while maintaining a consistent competitive edge. The club’s golden periods came in the late 1970s and early 2000s, when it dominated South American football with a blend of tactical organization and individual brilliance. Boca Juniors’ identity is deeply tied to the Libertadores, and their passionate fanbase views the tournament as the ultimate stage for glory. Their rivalry with River Plate, which has won four titles , adds another layer of intensity to the competition. River Plate’s triumphs, particularly in 2015 and 2018, highlight the club’s resurgence in the modern era and its ability to compete at the highest level. Uruguayan giants such as Peñarol and Nacional have also played a crucial role in the history of the Libertadores. Peñarol, one of the founding powers of the tournament, has won five titles and was the champion of the inaugural edition in 1960. Nacional, with multiple titles of its own, has consistently been a strong contender, contributing to Uruguay’s rich footballing heritage. In Brazil, clubs like Flamengo, Palmeiras, and São Paulo have emerged as dominant forces, particularly in recent decades. Brazilian clubs have enjoyed a period of sustained success in the 21st century, supported by financial strength, strong domestic leagues, and a continuous supply of talented players. While clubs provide the structure for success, managers are often the architects behind great teams. Among all coaches in Libertadores history, none has achieved more than Carlos Bianchi. Bianchi won the competition four times, first with Vélez Sarsfield in 1994 and then three times with Boca Juniors in 2000, 2001, and 2003 . His achievements are a testament to his tactical intelligence, leadership, and ability to build cohesive teams capable of performing under immense pressure. Bianchi’s Boca Juniors side is often regarded as one of the greatest teams in Libertadores history, known for its defensive solidity and clinical efficiency. Another legendary figure is Osvaldo Zubeldía, who led Estudiantes to three consecutive titles from 1968 to 1970 . Zubeldía was a pioneer of modern tactical approaches, emphasizing organization, discipline, and set-piece strategies. His influence extended beyond his own team, shaping the tactical evolution of South American football. Similarly, Luiz Felipe Scolari and Paulo Autuori are notable for winning the Libertadores with different clubs, demonstrating adaptability and sustained excellence . Edgardo Bauza also stands out for achieving this feat, including a historic victory with Ecuadorian club LDU Quito, highlighting the competition’s expanding geographic diversity.

In more recent times, managers such as Marcelo Gallardo have continued to shape the Libertadores narrative. Gallardo led River Plate to two titles in 2015 and 2018, establishing himself as one of the most successful modern coaches in South America . His teams are known for their attacking style, tactical flexibility, and resilience, qualities that have made River Plate a formidable opponent. The success of modern managers reflects the evolving nature of the tournament, where tactical sophistication and squad management are increasingly important. If managers design the blueprint for success, goalscorers provide the decisive moments that define matches and championships. The greatest goalscorer in Copa Libertadores history is Alberto Spencer, who scored 54 goals in the competition . Spencer’s record has stood for decades, a testament to his consistency and goal-scoring prowess. Playing primarily for Peñarol, he was instrumental in the club’s early successes and remains an iconic figure in South American football. His ability to perform on the biggest stage set a standard for future generations. Following Spencer are players such as Fernando Morena and Pedro Rocha, who scored 37 and 36 goals respectively . These players were central figures in the dominance of Uruguayan clubs during the early decades of the competition. Their goal-scoring exploits highlight the importance of individual brilliance in a tournament often decided by narrow margins. In more recent years, players like Gabriel Barbosa have climbed the rankings, reflecting the continued evolution of attacking talent in South America. Barbosa, known as “Gabigol,” has been a key figure for Flamengo, scoring crucial goals in multiple campaigns and becoming one of the modern faces of the Libertadores.

Other notable goalscorers include Daniel Onega, who holds the record for most goals in a single edition with 17 in 1966 . This achievement underscores the potential for individual players to dominate a single tournament, even if they do not top the all-time charts. Players such as Lucas Pratto and Miguel Borja, both with over 30 goals, demonstrate the longevity required to reach the upper echelons of the scoring rankings. Their careers reflect the modern reality of the Libertadores, where players often move between clubs and leagues, yet still leave a lasting impact on the competition. The interplay between clubs, managers, and players is what ultimately defines success in the Copa Libertadores. Great teams require visionary leadership and exceptional talent, while great players depend on strong systems and supportive environments. The dominance of clubs like Independiente and Boca Juniors would not have been possible without the contributions of legendary managers and prolific goalscorers. Similarly, the achievements of players like Alberto Spencer are inseparable from the teams and contexts in which they played. It is also important to recognize how the balance of power in the Libertadores has shifted over time. In the early decades, Uruguayan and Argentine clubs dominated, reflecting their strong footballing traditions and organizational structures. In recent years, Brazilian clubs have emerged as the dominant force, supported by financial investment and a large talent pool. This shift has influenced the profiles of successful managers and players, with Brazilian teams often attracting top talent from across the continent. Despite these changes, the competition remains open and unpredictable, allowing new teams and individuals to rise to prominence.

The Copa Libertadores is ultimately a competition where history and legacy carry immense weight. Records are not merely numbers; they are stories of triumph, perseverance, and excellence. The seven titles of Independiente, the four triumphs of Carlos Bianchi, and the 54 goals of Alberto Spencer are milestones that define the tournament’s identity. They serve as benchmarks for future generations, inspiring players, coaches, and clubs to strive for greatness. In conclusion, the history of the Copa Libertadores is shaped by its most successful teams, its greatest managers, and its most prolific goalscorers. These figures represent the pinnacle of achievement in South American club football, each contributing to the rich tapestry of the competition. From the dominance of Independiente to the tactical genius of Carlos Bianchi and the goal-scoring brilliance of Alberto Spencer, their legacies continue to resonate. As the Libertadores evolves, new names will undoubtedly emerge, but the achievements of these legends will remain a defining part of its enduring story.
